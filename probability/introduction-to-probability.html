MMXVIII.







TO HER, THE MOST WISE


NIKKI MONNINK.

 

        
 

    

Nikki,


  It is more than obvious, you remained one of my respectable rather parental figure, in all those
        tought times, and all those times when I rise, and times I soared. My admiration to your prowess,
        I dedicate this, my first work to you, with promise to conquer history, for you.


  No other person, is worthy to be my teacher in Mathematics, no woman and man shall dictate me,
        unless its you to whom I would listen. None, did I ever saw comparable to your intellect, nor
        shall I see on the face of this planet. With my first textbook I am dedicating to you, I shall
        promise you, my story, my conquests, my victories are incomplete if not I share my virtues with
        you. 

 
  You discovered me in those toughest times, when I could not express myself, you taught me how
         to walk, to talk, to dress, to speak. It is more than honour that I know the smartest person to
         exist on face of this planet. Let's write my story, together.


 MADAM,


May it please YOUR MAJESTY,


YOUR MAJESTY's most obedient and most dutiful subject and servant,


John Conduitt.

Advertisement.


Tho' The Chronology of Ancient Kingdoms amended, was writ by
  the Author many years since; yet he lately revis'd it, and was actually
  preparing it for the Press at the time of his death. But The Short
  Chronicle was never intended to be made public, and therefore was not
  so lately corrected by him. To this the Reader must impute it, if he
  shall find any places where the Short Chronicle does not
  accurately agree with the Dates assigned in the larger Piece. The Sixth
  Chapter was not copied out with the other Five, which makes it doubtful
  whether he intended to print it: but being found among his Papers, and
  evidently appearing to be a Continuation of the same Work, and (as such)
  abridg'd in the Short Chronicle; it was thought proper to be
  added.


Had the Great Author himself liv'd to publish this Work,
  there would have been no occasion for this Advertisement; But as it is,
  the Reader is desired to allow for such imperfections as are inseparable
  from Posthumous Pieces; and, in so great a number of proper names, to
  excuse some errors of the Press that have escaped.

The INTRODUCTION.


The Greek Antiquities are full of Poetical Fictions, because
  the Greeks wrote nothing in Prose, before the Conquest of
  Asia by Cyrus the Persian. Then Pherecydes
  Scyrius and Cadmus Milesius introduced the writing in Prose.
  Pherecydes Atheniensis, about the end of the Reign of Darius
  Hystaspis, wrote of Antiquities, and digested his work by
  Genealogies, and was reckoned one of the best Genealogers.
